Audit & Risk Mindset

Thinking in Bets: Making Smarter Decisions When You Don't Have All the Facts

by Annie Duke [Goodreads] [YouTube]

Why is probabilistic thinking essential for auditors?

Auditors operate in environments of incomplete information and time pressure. Duke argues we should evaluate decisions based on quality of process rather than outcomes alone. A good audit finding can be ignored by management; that doesn't make the audit bad.

  • Separate decision quality from outcome quality to avoid resulting bias
  • Build "truth-seeking pods" where colleagues challenge your assumptions
  • Ask "What would have to be true?" to stress-test audit conclusions
  • Use Bayesian updating: revise probabilities as new evidence emerges

Further Reading

  • Superforecasting by Philip Tetlock
  • The Signal and the Noise by Nate Silver

The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable

by Nassim Nicholas Taleb [Goodreads] [YouTube]

How should auditors think about tail risk?

Traditional audit models focus on "normal" risk distributions. Taleb shows that the most consequential events are the ones we don't predict. For financial institutions, this means stress-testing beyond regulatory scenarios and questioning models that assume stability.

  • Distinguish between Mediocristan (normal distributions) vs. Extremistan (power laws)
  • Build robustness through redundancy, not optimization alone
  • Use negative testing: identify what could break the system
  • Reward conservative estimates in risk models

Further Reading

  • Antifragile by Nassim Nicholas Taleb
  • When Genius Failed by Roger Lowenstein

The Checklist Manifesto: How to Get Things Right

by Atul Gawande [Goodreads] [YouTube]

Why do experts need checklists?

Gawande (a surgeon) proves that even highly skilled professionals fail due to complexity and time pressure — the exact environment of cross-enterprise audits. Checklists aren't about dumbing down work; they're about avoiding preventable errors.

  • Create Do-Confirm checklists (execute then verify) vs. Read-Do (step-by-step)
  • Keep audit checklists to 5–9 items per phase (cognitive load limits)
  • Test checklists in real scenarios and iterate based on failure modes
  • Empower teams to pause and flag when checklist reveals issues

Further Reading

  • To Engineer Is Human by Henry Petroski
  • Normal Accidents by Charles Perrow

Leadership & Organizations

Turn the Ship Around!: A True Story of Turning Followers into Leaders

by L. David Marquet [Goodreads] [YouTube]

How do you lead when you're not the smartest person in the room?

Marquet transformed the worst-performing submarine in the US Navy into the best by pushing authority to information rather than pulling information to authority. For audit leaders managing specialists across technology, compliance, and operations — this model is transformative.

  • Replace "I intend to..." with team members saying "I intend to..." (intent-based leadership)
  • Give control, create leaders: shift from compliance-driven to ownership-driven audits
  • Build technical competence AND organizational clarity in your team
  • Create mechanisms that make it safe to surface problems early

Further Reading

  • Extreme Ownership by Jocko Willink
  • Leaders Eat Last by Simon Sinek

Principles: Life and Work

by Ray Dalio [Goodreads] [YouTube]

How do you build systems that survive you?

Dalio built Bridgewater into the world's largest hedge fund through radical transparency and idea meritocracy. His approach to documenting principles — rather than relying on charisma — offers a blueprint for institutionalizing audit excellence.

  • Document your decision-making principles explicitly (create an "Audit Principles" playbook)
  • Embrace "believability-weighted decision-making" in risk assessments
  • Build feedback loops: dissect every audit to extract learnable principles
  • Separate ego from outcome: truth > being right

Further Reading

  • Principles for Dealing with the Changing World Order by Ray Dalio
  • The Fifth Discipline by Peter Senge

Multipliers: How the Best Leaders Make Everyone Smarter

by Liz Wiseman [Goodreads] [YouTube]

Are you amplifying or diminishing your team's intelligence?

Wiseman's research shows that Multiplier leaders extract 2x more capability from their teams than Diminishers. In audit teams with deep technical specialists, being the "genius with a thousand helpers" kills effectiveness.

  • Talent Magnet: Attract A-players by creating growth opportunities
  • Liberator: Create space for hard truths, not just confirmation bias
  • Challenger: Seed big questions, don't dictate answers
  • Debate Maker: Rigorous debate on audit findings before finalizing
  • Investor: Give ownership, not orders

Further Reading

  • Impact Players by Liz Wiseman
  • The Culture Code by Daniel Coyle

AI in Compliance

The Alignment Problem: Machine Learning and Human Values

by Brian Christian [Goodreads] [YouTube]

How do we ensure AI systems do what we actually want?

Christian explores the central challenge of AI governance: aligning machine behavior with human values. For auditors evaluating AI/ML models in credit decisioning, fraud detection, or algorithmic trading — this is the foundational text.

  • Understand reward hacking: models optimize what you measure, not what you mean
  • Test for fairness across protected classes (disparate impact analysis)
  • Implement "red team" exercises: adversarial testing of AI audit tools
  • Build interpretability requirements into model governance frameworks

Further Reading

  • Weapons of Math Destruction by Cathy O'Neil
  • Superintelligence by Nick Bostrom

Co-Intelligence: Living and Working with AI

by Ethan Mollick [Goodreads] [YouTube]

How should professionals integrate AI into workflows?

Mollick (Wharton professor) provides the most practical framework for working alongside AI rather than fearing displacement. For auditors building RAG systems and testing MCP protocols, this is essential reading.

  • Treat AI as co-worker, coach, consultant, and simulator in audit workflows
  • Use AI for hypothesis generation, not just execution (reverse the creative process)
  • Build "centaur" teams: humans for judgment, AI for pattern recognition
  • Document AI usage in audit evidence (maintain the chain of custody)

Further Reading

  • Prediction Machines by Ajay Agrawal
  • Human + Machine by Paul Daugherty

Trustworthy AI: A Business Guide for Navigating Trust and Ethics in AI

by Beena Ammanath [Goodreads] [YouTube]

What does responsible AI governance look like in practice?

Ammanath (former Deloitte AI lead) translates AI ethics principles into actionable frameworks for financial services. This is the playbook for building board-ready AI governance documentation.

  • Establish AI Ethics Councils with cross-functional representation
  • Create Model Risk Management frameworks (SR 11-7 compliance)
  • Build explainability requirements into vendor due diligence
  • Implement continuous monitoring for model drift and bias

Further Reading

  • The AI Ladder by Rob Thomas
  • AI Ethics by Mark Coeckelbergh

Finance & Decision-Making

Thinking, Fast and Slow

by Daniel Kahneman [Goodreads] [YouTube]

How do cognitive biases distort audit judgment?

Kahneman's Nobel Prize-winning research reveals how System 1 (fast, intuitive) and System 2 (slow, analytical) thinking lead to predictable errors. For my dissertation on anchoring bias in auditing, this is the foundation.

  • Anchoring: First number presented skews estimates (manage reference points)
  • Availability Heuristic: Recent audit failures dominate risk assessment
  • Confirmation Bias: Seeking evidence that supports pre-existing conclusions
  • Planning Fallacy: Systematically underestimating audit timelines
  • Implement pre-mortem analysis: assume the audit failed, work backwards to causes

Further Reading

  • Misbehaving by Richard Thaler
  • Nudge by Richard Thaler & Cass Sunstein

Fooled by Randomness: The Hidden Role of Chance in Life and in the Markets

by Nassim Nicholas Taleb [Goodreads] [YouTube]

How do we distinguish skill from luck in financial outcomes?

Taleb argues that humans are terrible at recognizing randomness — we create narratives to explain outcomes that were largely chance. Critical for auditors evaluating trading strategies, credit models, and operational performance.

  • Survivorship bias: only analyze the funds/models that survived, ignore those that failed
  • Assess risk by alternative histories: what could have happened, not just what did
  • Separate process quality from outcome: good controls + bad luck ≠ bad audit
  • Build redundancy into critical controls (Taleb's "barbell strategy")

Further Reading

  • The Drunkard's Walk by Leonard Mlodinow
  • Against the Gods by Peter Bernstein

More Than You Know: Finding Financial Wisdom in Unconventional Places

by Michael Mauboussin [Goodreads]

How can multidisciplinary thinking improve financial analysis?

Mauboussin (former Chief Investment Strategist at Legg Mason) draws from biology, psychology, and complexity science to illuminate finance. For auditors, this cross-pollination of ideas is transformative.

  • Apply complex adaptive systems thinking to interconnected risks
  • Use base rates (outside view) before adjusting for specifics (inside view)
  • Understand reversion to the mean in performance metrics
  • Build probabilistic thinking into audit report conclusions

Further Reading

  • The Success Equation by Michael Mauboussin
  • Margin of Safety by Seth Klarman

Business Biography

The Everything Store: Jeff Bezos and the Age of Amazon

by Brad Stone [Goodreads] [YouTube]

What principles drive long-term value creation?

Bezos built Amazon on customer obsession, long-term thinking, and willingness to be misunderstood. For entrepreneurs balancing real estate portfolios with AI ventures, this is the masterclass in systems-driven growth.

  • Work backwards from customer needs (not forwards from capabilities)
  • Institutionalize innovation through "two-pizza teams" (autonomy + accountability)
  • Build mechanisms that scale: decision frameworks, not heroic effort
  • Accept that bold bets require patient capital (AWS took 7 years to profit)

Further Reading

  • The Innovators by Walter Isaacson
  • Steve Jobs by Walter Isaacson

Shoe Dog: A Memoir by the Creator of Nike

by Phil Knight [Goodreads] [YouTube]

How do you build something from nothing?

Knight's story is one of relentless resourcefulness — selling shoes from his car trunk to building a global icon. The lesson: entrepreneurship is messy, scary, and requires irrational commitment.

  • Start before you're ready (Knight had no business experience when he launched)
  • Build a team bonded by mission, not just comp (the "Buttfaces" culture)
  • Cash flow is oxygen: even great businesses die from running out of cash
  • Bet on people, not plans: Nike's success came from trusting unconventional talent

Further Reading

  • The Hard Thing About Hard Things by Ben Horowitz
  • Zero to One by Peter Thiel

Sam Walton: Made in America

by Sam Walton [Goodreads] [YouTube]

How did a small-town merchant build the world's largest retailer?

Walton's principles — frugality, operational excellence, and employee empowerment — transformed retail. For Malik LLC's real estate operations, these lessons in lean operations and delegation are invaluable.

  • Control expenses relentlessly (every dollar wasted is a dollar not invested)
  • Push responsibility down: store managers knew their markets better than HQ
  • Listen to frontline employees: "Our best ideas come from delivery and stock boys"
  • Outwork the competition: Walton visited stores weekly until his death

Further Reading

  • The Wal-Mart Effect by Charles Fishman
  • Pour Your Heart Into It by Howard Schultz
